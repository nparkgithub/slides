Acts 5:1–11

Ananias & Sapphira

Hypocrisy, Holiness, and the Holy Spirit

- Welcome everyone. Today we're looking at a sobering and very important story from the early church. - It's found in Acts 5, right after a beautiful picture of unity and generosity. - Acts 4 ends with the positive example of Barnabas. Acts 5 begins with the contrasting story of Ananias and Sapphira. - This passage isn't just about money; it's about integrity, the holiness of God, and the reality of spiritual warfare in the church.

Context: Acts 4 → Acts 5

  • Acts 4 ends with Barnabas selling a field.
  • Barnabas gave sincerely.
  • Acts 5 begins with “But...”
  • Ananias and Sapphira imitate the action.
  • Their motive is different.
Barnabas Ananias & Sapphira
Sincere Hypocritical
Truth Deception
God's glory Human praise
- Luke, the author of Acts, is a master storyteller. He deliberately places these two accounts side-by-side. - Barnabas is an example of grace-filled giving. Ananias and Sapphira are an example of pride-filled posturing. - The core issue is not the amount of money given, but the motive of the heart. - Barnabas sought to serve the community and glorify God. Ananias and Sapphira sought praise and reputation for themselves.

Acts 5:1–2

Ananias and Sapphira sold property and kept back part of the proceeds.
  • Keeping part was not the sin.
  • The property belonged to them.
  • The money belonged to them.
  • The sin was pretending the gift was the full amount.

Hypocrisy = appearing more devoted than reality.

- Let's be clear: their sin was not keeping some of the money. Peter says later, "Didn't it belong to you before it was sold? And after it was sold, wasn't the money at your disposal?" - The sin was the lie. They pretended to give the entire amount while secretly holding back a portion. - This is hypocrisy: the gap between our outward appearance and our inward reality. They wanted the reputation of sacrificial givers without the actual sacrifice. - This connects to the story of Achan in Joshua 7. The same Greek word for "kept back" (nosphizomai) is used in the Septuagint (the Greek OT) to describe Achan's sin. It's a sin of deceit against God's community.

Acts 5:3–4

“Why has Satan filled your heart to lie to the Holy Spirit?”

Satan & Holy Spirit

  • Acts 2 & 4: believers filled with the Spirit.
  • Acts 5: Satan fills Ananias' heart.
  • Spiritual battle within the church.
  • Ananias remains responsible.
- Peter immediately identifies the spiritual source of the temptation: Satan. - But notice, he doesn't absolve Ananias of responsibility. He asks, "how is it that you have conceived this deed in your heart?" - This is a key principle: Satan can tempt and influence, but we are the ones who make the choice to sin. - The heart, which should be filled with the Holy Spirit (as in Acts 2 & 4), was instead filled with Satan's deception.

The Holy Spirit is God

Verse 3 Verse 4
Lied to the Holy Spirit Lied to God
  • One of the clearest affirmations of the deity of the Holy Spirit.
  • To deceive God's people is ultimately to deceive God.
- This is one of the most powerful verses in the Bible for affirming the deity of the Holy Spirit. - In verse 3, Peter says Ananias lied to the Holy Spirit. In verse 4, he says he lied to God. - For Peter, there is no distinction. The Holy Spirit is fully God. - This means that when we sin against the church, when we bring deceit into the community of believers, we are not just sinning against people. We are sinning against God Himself, who dwells in His people by His Spirit.

Acts 5:5–6

The Death of Ananias

  • Peter exposed the sin.
  • God executed judgment.
  • Immediate death.
  • Great fear came upon all.

Comparable moments:

  • Achan (Joshua 7)
  • Nadab & Abihu (Leviticus 10)
- The consequence is shocking and immediate. God strikes Ananias dead. - This is a foundational moment for the new covenant community, the Church. God is setting a standard of holiness from the very beginning. - This is not the only time we see this. Think of Nadab and Abihu in Leviticus 10, who offered unauthorized fire, or Achan in Joshua 7. - At these pivotal moments in redemptive history, God sometimes acts with dramatic judgment to purify His people and establish His holiness. The result is "great fear"—a healthy, reverent awe of God.

Acts 5:7–8

Sapphira's Opportunity

  • Three hours later.
  • She does not know what happened.
  • Peter gives her a chance to tell the truth.
  • She repeats the lie.

God gives opportunities for repentance.

- Three hours pass. This is a significant detail. Sapphira had time. She was not caught in the immediate heat of the moment. - Peter, led by the Spirit, gives her a direct opportunity to confess. He doesn't trap her; he tests her. "Tell me, is this the price you and Ananias got for the land?" - This is an act of mercy. It's a chance to break from the deception. But she doubles down on the lie. She is a full partner in the sin.

Acts 5:9–10

Testing the Spirit

“How could you conspire to test the Spirit of the Lord?”
  • Testing God = seeing how far disobedience can go.
  • Presuming upon God's patience.
  • Assuming sin can remain hidden.

Result: Sapphira receives the same judgment.

- Peter's language is crucial: "How could you conspire to test the Spirit of the Lord?" - What does it mean to "test" God? It means to deliberately sin while presuming upon His grace and patience. It's seeing how much you can get away with. - They were essentially daring God, assuming He either didn't know or didn't care about their hypocrisy. They were tragically wrong. - This echoes the command in Deuteronomy 6:16, which Jesus quotes to Satan in Matthew 4: "Do not put the Lord your God to the test."

Acts 5:11

The First Mention of "Church"

  • Greek: Ekklesia
  • Called-out assembly
  • Refers to the Jerusalem church
  • Great fear came upon everyone

Fear = reverent awe of God's holiness.

- This is the first time the word "church" (ekklesia) appears in the book of Acts. - The judgment on Ananias and Sapphira purifies the church and creates a holy fear, not just within the church but also among those outside who heard about it. - This "fear" is not just being scared. It's a profound, healthy reverence. It's the recognition that God is holy, He is present, and He is not to be trifled with.

Why Was God So Severe?

  • Protection of the early church.
  • Preservation of trust.
  • Defense against hypocrisy.
  • Warning to future believers.

God was establishing a holy community.

- Why such a harsh judgment? We don't see this happen every time someone is hypocritical in church today. - This was a unique, foundational moment. The church was in its infancy. God was establishing its DNA. - The integrity of the community was at stake. If hypocrisy was allowed to take root, it would have poisoned the church from the inside out. - This act served as a powerful and permanent warning for all future generations of believers about the seriousness of sin and the importance of integrity.

Were They Genuine Believers?

  • Luke treats them as part of the church.
  • They belonged to the believing community.
  • True believers can commit serious sins.
  • Spiritual appearance ≠ spiritual health.

The issue is hypocrisy, not church membership.

- This is a difficult question. The text doesn't explicitly say. They were part of the community. - However, it's a stark reminder that being a member of a church and even participating in its activities does not automatically mean one's heart is right with God. - It also shows that genuine believers are capable of committing serious sin (see 1 Corinthians 5). The Bible is realistic about our fallen nature. - The key takeaway is that our spiritual appearance can be deceiving. God is concerned with the reality of our hearts.

Satan's Strategy

Acts 4 Acts 5
External attack Internal attack
Persecution Hypocrisy
Threats Deception

Satan failed to stop the church from outside and attacked from within.

- This marks a crucial shift in Satan's tactics. - In Acts 4, the attack was external: persecution, threats, and imprisonment. This failed; in fact, it made the church bolder. - In Acts 5, the attack becomes internal: deception and hypocrisy. This is often a far more dangerous strategy. - External pressure tends to unite and strengthen the church. Internal corruption divides and destroys it from within.

Key Greek Words

νοσφίζομαι (nosphizomai) v. 2, "kept back". Implies deceitful holding back. Same word used for Achan's sin in Joshua 7 (LXX).
ψεύδομαι (pseudomai) v. 3, "to lie". The core of the sin was deception against God and His community.
πειράζω (peirazō) v. 9, "to test". To deliberately sin while presuming on God's grace and patience.
ἐκκλησία (ekklēsia) v. 11, "church". The first use of this word in Acts. A "called-out assembly".
φόβος (phobos) v. 5, 11, "fear". Not just terror, but a profound and healthy reverence for a holy God.
- A quick look at the original language gives us even deeper insight. - **Nosphizomai**: This isn't just "keeping," it's "embezzling" or "purloining." It directly links this story to Achan's sin in the Old Testament, who stole what was devoted to God. - **Pseudomai**: The root of their sin was a lie. They weren't just misrepresenting a number; they were misrepresenting their hearts before God. - **Peirazō**: "To test" the Spirit. This is a deliberate challenge to God's authority and omniscience. They were seeing what they could get away with. - **Ekklēsia**: It's significant that the first use of "church" in Acts is right after this event. God purifies His assembly at its very foundation. - **Phobos**: This is the proper response to witnessing God's holiness and power. It's not a cowering fear, but a reverent awe that leads to worship and obedience.

Application for Believers Today

  • God cares about motives.
  • Avoid spiritual image management.
  • Practice honesty before God.
  • Confess hidden sin quickly.
  • Do not test God's patience.
  • Value integrity over reputation.
- So what does this mean for us, 2000 years later? - God still cares deeply about the motives of our hearts, not just our outward actions. - We must fight the temptation to manage our spiritual image, to appear more holy or generous than we are. - The call is to practice radical honesty before God and with a trusted few in our community. Confess sin quickly. - Ultimately, we are called to value integrity before God far more than our reputation before people.

Personal Reflection

  1. Do I care more about God's approval or people's approval?
  2. Am I pretending to be more spiritual than I really am?
  3. Is there hidden sin I need to confess?
  4. Does my private life match my public image?
  5. Am I walking in truth before God?
- Let's take a moment to reflect on these questions personally. - This isn't about public shaming, but private, honest self-examination before God. - (Pause for a minute of silence). - The goal of this story is not to make us live in terror, but to lead us to repentance and a more authentic walk with God.

Main Message

God is not asking us to be impressive.

God is asking us to be genuine.

Acts 5 is a warning against hypocrisy and a call to integrity before a holy God.

- The main message of this passage is simple but profound. - God is not impressed by our performance. He is looking for our hearts. He desires truth in the inward parts. - Let's close in prayer, asking God to cultivate in us a spirit of humility, truthfulness, and genuine integrity.
